Description of Our Model

We had to consider several parameters on approching this problem.


  1. We picked Twitter as a platform bceause it appeared to be easier to approach among the popular social media netwrorks, thanks to it's open and free API. We hope that this work can be extended to more personal social media networks.

  2. We experimented with various kinds of word embeddings, as this is a fundamental decision can introduce large variations in accuracies. We opted against using existing Word2Vec / GloVe word vectors, because these are trained on a Wikipedia or a news corpus, and do not lend themselves well to user generated content, the kind that is usually found on social media sites.

  3. Finally, we were confused about what architecture to go with, becaus there exists various deep learning architectures, and each of them have their own specific uses, advantages and disadvantages. We had to look for an architecture that had proven to be reliable on content like tweets, and it had to be sensitive enough to learn the subtle differences between valid disaster tweets and non-disaster tweets.
    We first chose to model our architecture after a very recent and well researched research paper titled Selective Expression For Event Coreference Resolution on Twitter [2019] from Wenhan Chao et. al., published from the School of Computer Science and Engineering, Biejing. The paper can be found here.

    We chose this paper as it had a well tested architecture that had previosly been utilised to study tweets, and it had shown great results. Unfortunately, as we feared, a deep learning approach added a very large overhead in both testing and training time. The task at hand demanded a very quick method of gauging disasters, owing to the volatile nature of Twitter, and the speed at which content is uploaded on the website. Furthermore, due to the still new nature of deep learning language modelling architectures, the results obtained were not drastically higher than using ML methods.

    So we have gone ahead with a logistic regression machine learning model, that we worked together and programmed from scratch.

Monitoring Disasters and Accidents through Twitter

Twitter, and other social media sites have become important communication channel in times of emergencies. As smartphones become more ubiquitous, it becomes imperative to monitor these outlets.


Law enforcement agencies, disaster relief organizations and news agencies are interested in programmatically monitoring Twitter. These become quick methods of identifying smaller scale disasters and risks.


Unfortunately, it’s usually a difficult task to correctly identify a valid disaster tweet.


We aim to use state-of-the-art NLP models to help disaster authorities identify occurrences of disasters immediately, by scouring social media for posts by citizens caught in a disaster. While local governments and relief organizations can measure a community’s ability to respond to a disaster or measure its impacts after, they cannot monitor the effects in real time. Several studies have shown that supplementing social media data can be a real advantage to first responders. Depending on the performance of the model, it may even be possible to deploy this aspect of the project in helping disaster management authorities in the country.
